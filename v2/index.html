Overview

Single-particle cryo-EM turns hundreds of thousands of noisy movie frames of a frozen protein into a three-dimensional density map. Getting there is a long, judgment-heavy workflow: align and average the movies, estimate each micrograph's defocus, pick particles out of the noise, sort them into 2-D classes, throw out the junk, build an initial 3-D model, and refine it to high resolution. Most steps carry decisions an expert makes by eye and by experience, and a poor call early silently caps the quality of everything downstream. Our goal is to measure how well an AI agent runs that entire loop. We build an environment in which the agent operates a live cryoSPARC pipeline end to end — its action space and the full per-run rollouts are laid out on the Tasks page — and a grader that scores the procedural correctness of the run and the quality of its outcome relative to an expert workflow and the published 3D reconstruction, defined subscore by subscore on the Grader page.

The proving ground is T20S, the 20S proteasome (EMPIAR-10025): the standard public cryoSPARC tutorial dataset, with a published map and atomic model (EMD-6287 / PDB 6BDF) to grade against. The cryoSPARC tutorial supplies a publicly available, expert-verified workflow for this dataset; we ran the T20S reconstruction ourselves by hand, following that tutorial, and use the result as a proxy for the expert workflow the agent is graded against. T20S lets us build and validate every piece — the agent's action space, the reconstruction rollouts, and each grader metric — against a known, public answer key. It is also deliberately easy: the particle is small, highly symmetric, and well-behaved, and the tutorial hands the agent several decisions that are genuinely hard in real work. The per-run, per-subscore scores on this dataset are reported on the Benchmark page, and the Env spec page casts the whole environment as a response to Anthropic's scientific-evaluation-environments RFP — its task, data, signals, difficulty, and access.

T20S is a precursor to the dataset the pilot targets: research-grade data from the Ward lab at Scripps. There the specimens are antibody–antigen complexes (HIV-immunogen structures) — larger, flexible, lower-symmetry particles where the reconstruction is materially harder, the right decisions are genuinely uncertain rather than handed over by a tutorial, and a poor early call visibly degrades the final map. These structures are published, so the same kind of deposited ground truth the grader already uses carries over; what is non-public is the full reconstruction pipeline behind each paper — the raw movies, particle selections, intermediate maps, and the model-building and refinement logs with their parameter choices and reasoning. Because the public anchor and that non-public pipeline are the same structural system, the environment scales from the public tutorial to research data on one continuous workflow — the same action space and the same grader, pointed at a problem with real room to separate good science from bad. The Next phase page details that scale-up.
